Punctuation

Ampersands and plus signs

Use “and” instead of an ampersand or plus sign, unless they’re part of an official title or company name.

Bulleted lists

If you're introducing your list with some text, follow that text with a colon.

If the items in your list are full sentences, punctuate them like sentences, with a capital letter and a full stop.

Offshore tax havens are bad for the world:

  • Tax revenues that are vital for public services are siphoned off.
  • Rich people become richer, while poor people become poorer.
  • Corruption goes unpunished.
  • Democracy is undermined by a lack of accountability.

If the items in your list are not full sentences, punctuate your list as you would a normal sentence: follow each item with a comma, except the penultimate item, which should end with "and", and the last item, which should end with a full stop. And don't start them with capital letters (unless they are proper nouns, as in the example below).

Offshore tax havens include:

  • Bermuda,
  • Luxembourg,
  • Cayman Islands and
  • Singapore.

If the items in your list are not full sentences, but have multiples clauses, end them with semicolons, except the penultimate item, which should end with "; and" and the last item, which should end with a full stop.

Tax havens:

  • corrupt markets, concealing insider dealing and supporting aggressive tax dodging by multinational companies;
  • create a private world of secrecy, impunity and power for rich elites; and
  • help the world’s wealthiest keep a disproportionate share of the benefits of globalisation, by preventing the rest of us from seeing how much they own.

Colons

Use a colon between two sentences, or parts of sentences, where the first introduces a proposition that is resolved by the second.

Also use a colon to introduce a quotation.

Commas

We don’t use the serial comma (sometimes called the Oxford comma) except where it is needed to clarify meaning. In a list of three or more items, don’t include a comma before the conjunction.

Dashes

When offsetting a phrase with dashes you should use the en dash (–), with a space on either side of the dash.

Although we advocate using words rather than symbols, in some contexts you may use a shorter dash to convey a range of numbers. For example, both 10-20 hectares and 10 to 20 hectares are acceptable.

We don’t use em dashes (— extra long dashes that typically appear without spaces on either side).

Hyphens

Use when more than one word makes up an adjective.

Muddle-headed thinking, carbon-neutral projects.

If a compound’s meaning is established and the meaning is clear and unambiguous then a hyphen is unnecessary.

Civil rights movement, financial services sector.

Also, use a hyphen where not using one would be ambiguous:

She had a concealed-weapons permit.

Compound nouns should generally be written as one word.

Thinktank, shortlist

Quotes

These quotations are correctly punctuated:

“Would you like to make a banner?” he asked.

“I hate banners,” she said. “You know I hate banners.”

He paused before saying: “Banners are not something people should hate.”

Spaces

Sentences should always be separated by a single space. Never by two spaces.

Slashes

Avoid using the slash / symbol. Replace it with words or commas as appropriate.
